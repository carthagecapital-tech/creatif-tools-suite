§ 06Help & Reference

§ 01How to Use

Step 1: Fill in your recipe details. Enter a name, choose a category, and set your batch yield (e.g., "this recipe makes 12 cookies").

Step 2: Add ingredients one by one. For each ingredient, enter the quantity you purchased, what you paid, the quantity used in your recipe, and any yield loss percentage (waste from peeling, trimming, etc.).

Step 3: Set "I want to make" to scale the batch. All ingredient quantities and costs rescale automatically.

Step 4: Fill in labor hours, your target hourly rate, packaging cost per unit, kitchen overhead, booth fee (if selling at market), expected sales units, and mileage.

Step 5: Adjust the target margin slider to see how different pricing affects your profit and true hourly wage.

Step 6: Click Save Recipe to store it. View saved recipes, compare two recipes side-by-side, and analyze trends in the Analytics view.

§ 03Yield Loss Guide

Yield loss accounts for waste when preparing ingredients — peeling, trimming, cooking loss, and unusable portions. Enter the percentage of loss for each ingredient.

Onions20% (peeling, trimming)
Butter10% (pan residue)
Carrots15% (peeling, tops)
Potatoes18% (peeling, eyes)
Apples15% (coring, peeling)
Citrus25% (zest only, juicing)
Fresh herbs30% (stems, bruised leaves)
Chocolate5% (melting residue)
Flour (sifted)5% (sifting loss)
Meat (bone-in)25-35% (bone, trim)
Coffee beans15% (chaff, grind loss)
Nuts (shelled)5% (shell fragments, dust)

Tip: When yield loss reaches 100%, the effective quantity equals the scaled recipe quantity (no infinite cost).

§ 06Pricing Strategy for Cottage Food Sellers

Know your true costs. Factor in every ingredient, the time you spend (pay yourself first!), packaging, overhead, and market fees. Use the true hourly wage to ensure you're paying yourself fairly.

Start at 40% margin. A 40% target margin is a good starting point for cottage foods. Adjust based on your local market — gourmet or specialty items can command 50-60%.

Account for booth fees. If your market charges $40/week and you sell 50 units, each unit carries an $0.80 booth cost. Use the event amortization fields to see the real impact.

Don't forget mileage. The IRS mileage rate (~$0.67/mi) adds up fast. A 20-mile round trip costs $13.40 — that's real money coming out of your profit.

Price psychology matters. Round prices up to friendly numbers ($8.00 instead of $7.83). Customers perceive quality at whole-dollar price points.

Test different batch sizes. Use the Compare view to see how scaling up affects your per-unit cost and hourly wage. Bigger batches often mean better margins.

Track everything. Save every recipe iteration. Use analytics to spot which recipes earn you the most per hour — focus on those.
